Step 6: Communicate (Inform Decisions)

  Overview and Goals

By this step in the process, the team has done significant work together and it is crucial to get the word out in ways that resonate with decision-makers and stakeholders. Careful targeting of particular audiences should drive the creation of products. Opportunities to present results to decision-makers should be created, seized, and thoughtfully delivered. If well-coordinated, reports detailing the results can be incorporated directly into planning documents (Clarke et al. 2013, Santa Cruz IRWM Plan 2014). In some instances, it is important to publish peer-reviewed papers to help establish the credibility of the science (Arkema et al. 2014, Langridge et al. 2014) and thus gain the support and trust of internal science advisors and the broader scientific community. Also, communication with stakeholders can be key: simple diagrams and interactive maps can allow non-technical users to explore and understand results (Belize ICZM Plan, Coastal Habitats Defend U.S. Communities). Appropriate communication can lead to transparency, a shared understanding among diverse actors, and an increased likelihood of action.


The following synthesis products and strategies can get you closer to some communication goals:
  • Create syntheses of the results (e.g, reports, executive summaries, peer-reviewed papers, presentations, interactive maps) tailored to intended audiences
  • Share findings, and the ways in which they can inform planning and action, with the broader community
 

  Tips

  • Use appropriate media to communicate findings to different audiences (e.g., technical report for scientists vs. executive summary for government officials)
  • Communication is two-way; be a good listener and develop products that meet the needs of your audience
  • Don't use jargon (including, in most instances, the phrase "ecosystem services"). Use the terms that make sense to your stakeholders and decision-makers.
  • Tell stories. Human brains are wired to look for and remember a narrative. Find the stories and tell them well.
  • A central challenge here is that we are working to make implicit connections explicit. Don't leave people with the message that "it is complicated". Rather, leave them with the message that "we can make better decisions if we think this through differently".
  • Be honest about the science and underlying uncertainty (Refsgaard 2006, Walker et al. 2003)
  • Consider building an online repository (e.g., mapping portal and download) to archive and dissementate spatial data
  • Organizing a workshop to share results can be a good mechanism for feedback, drive the future evolution of the work (see Iterate step), and increase buy-in

  Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What kinds of information do decision-makers want to hear?
A: Decision-making is exceedingly complex. People have to balance multiple objectives, and need to gain the support of their stakeholders. For the most part, they want information that they can readily connect to the decisions at-hand. Thus, it is essential to involve those you hope to influence in your process from the beginning, so that you know how to connect your results to their needs. When co-created, information is more likely to be legitimate, credible, and salient (e.g., Cash et al. 2003, McKenzie et al. 2014).

Q: Do decision-makers always want monetary valuation of ecosystem services?
A: No. It's not always about the money. Biophysical outputs such as the area of erosion along the coastline can be just as meaningful, as can numbers of people or poor families at risk from hazards (Arkema et al. 2013, using data from the American Community Survey). In particular, when cultural services, such as recreation or opportunities for culturally important activities are involved, monetary valuation can be unnecessary while in other cases it provides additional useful context (e.g., Arkema et al. 2015, Keeler et al. 2015). In many cases it is preferable to report outcomes directly in terms of impacts on human health or livelihoods (Ruckelshaus et al. 2013) - something done more successfully in the academic literature than in practice (e.g., Keeler et al. 2012, Myers et al. 2013) - but something to strive for in this kind of work.

Belize

Communicating and applying results in Belize was multi-faceted, including a formal planning document, executive summary, peer-reviewed publications and online maps, videos and brochures. All synthesis products and communication materials were tailored to specific audiences and decision-makers.



  • The Belize Integrated Coastal Zone Management Plan (ICZMP) offers a legal framework for implementing a spatial zoning plan to ensure a sustainable future for Belize's coasts and oceans supported by thriving local communities and a vibrant economy.
  • Peer-reviewed publications can build credibility within the scientific community.
  • Online analytical tools can provide clear, targeted information to planners, reserve managers, and other decision-makers in Belize without requiring technical expertise.
  • This online mapping portal enabled citizens of Belize to explore zoning recommendations in greater detail than the static maps included within the ICZM plan.




California

Based on three years of research and community engagement, the Center for Ocean Solutions and Natural Capital Project have developed a methodology that informs climate adaptation and development decisions in California coastal counties. From 2010–2012, our team engaged with Monterey and Santa Cruz counties to inform regional water resource planners about the role of natural habitats in reducing the vulnerability of critical water infrastructure to sea level rise and storms (Langridge et al. 2014). As a result, coastal planners have selected nature-based protection strategies including a dune restoration effort to protect vital water infrastructure in the Salinas watershed. We currently engage with coastal planners in Monterey, Santa Cruz, Sonoma and Marin counties to inform their local coastal program planning updates. A major emphasis of this engagement is to provide science and policy expertise to support coastal planners with integrating the full suite of benefits provided by natural habitats into adaptation strategy development, prioritization and selection.



  • Peer-reviewed publications like this article in Ocean & Coastal Management by Langridge and colleagues led to dune restoration in cental California.
  • This one-page interim report is one example of how our partner, the Center for Ocean Solutions, communicated early findings to stakeholders in Sonoma County, California.
  • Peer-reviewed publications like this article in Ocean & Coastal Management by Langridge and colleagues led to dune restoration in cental California.
  • The team contributed adaptation strategies to the 2014 Santa Cruz IRWM Plan.
